Max Georgopoulos

I made a video series at Wavy called Field Trip to uncover Toronto's hidden gems.

Imagine Bon Appetit and a David Dobrik vlog had a baby. That's Field Trip.

I was the...

  • Producer (told coworkers I'd film them at lunch)
  • Director (yelled at coworkers)
  • Star (when I yelled at coworkers too much)
  • Editor (tried to make it watchable)
  • Cinematographer (shot it on my Pixel 3a)

I basically did everything. It was a startup after all.

It was super fun. I was making every shot up as I went along. Lunches became ~2.5 hours (intentional?)

Here are couple things I learned:

  • Make fast-paced content. The best videos are fast - almost jarring. When I started Field Trip, everyone was talking about David Dobrik. I studied his videos and noticed something: They're fast, almost jarring. He'll sacrifice continuity, quality everything for entertainment. Like good design, cut everything that's not entertaining or relevent to the story. Our attention is short - make your cuts shorter.
  • Every clip is gold. Martin Scorsesi's teacher said that ever shot is a treasure. A shot could initially be garbage but in the edit is exactly what you need to tell the story.
  • Create spontaneously. The best moments in Field Trip weren't planned. A whole episode came from a squirrel trying to eat our food.
  • Story > Quality. I filmed on a phone... VERTICALLY... and people still loved it. Good stories compensate for everything. Sometimes, slightly worse quality + great story makes you feel closer to the star (call it the Casey Neistat Effect).
  • Contraints = Creativity. For each video, I had to turn ~30 low-quality shots into 2 minutes of high-quality entertainment. I had to be ultra creative in the edit, which lead to Field Trip's edit-heavy style. Without contraints, you lean towards normal.
  • Don't use IGTV. No-one wants to watch a vertical video for more than 30 seconds (unless it's really good).
